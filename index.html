Hypersonic Technology Supplier Cyber Risk Assessment

Cover Figure

This mini-project aims to analyze hypersonic technology supplier risk from the contract data of USASpending. Major technology contracts are normally awarded to industry-leading companies (later referred to as “primary contractors”), and they will delegate some parts of a contract to subcontractors. Given the assumption that primary contractors have more resources and protection in cyber security, this project’s focus is on their subcontractors, who are more vulnerable to cyber-attack. In essence, it will help NSA rank hypersonic technology subcontractors, based on their risk attributes from contract data.

Analytics

Subcontractors Are Scatted Across the US

Figure 4 shows two of the largest primary contractors are located in California, and another large one is located in Alabama. These large companies delegate some parts of their contract to subcontractors near them, and other parts to subcontractors across the country. Diverse location of subcontractors alleviates geographical risk.

There Are Four Dominating Primary Contractors

Figure 5 shows Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Torch Technologies, and Aeroject Rocketdyne sign the largest number of primary contracts with the government, and delegate them to subcontractors. They are the center of the hypersonic contract network. Most companies don't have access to primary contracts.

Most Subcontractors Only Supply One Primary Contractor

It can be seen from Figure 6 that some primary contractors can also be subcontracts, but they are not this project's targets, since they are assumed to have better protection in cyber security. Very few subcontractors supply more than one primary contractor.

There is No Monopoly on Hypersonic Technology that Only One Single Subcontractor Can Provide

After applying some NLP techniques including lemmatizing, pos-tagging, and Tf-Idf on each subcontract's description, some words jump out as rare and important. These words are “accelerometer”, “amplifier”, “antenna”, “transducers”, “university”, etc.

However, this project can find more than one subcontractor with description having the above words. There is no word only appearing in one subcontractor's description, meaning no subcontractor has a unique technology. Subcontractors may have more delicate differences than they show in these contracts' descriptions, and that analysis requires more information and domain knowledge.

Conclusion

From the geographical analysis, we find subcontractors are scatted across the US. From the network analysis, we find there are four major hypersonic companies having access to primary contracts, and they delegate that to different subcontractors.

We find we can use the number of subcontracts, funding amount, and the number of supplied primary contractors as quantitative attributes to prioritize cyber risk. Most of the subcontractors have little funding and only supply one primary contractor, while a few have a lot of funding and supply more than one primary contractor. Among them, Pacific Scientific Energetic Materials Company and Ensign-Bickford Aerospace & Defense Company are the most important ones.

Finally, NLP methods help us find that no subcontractor has a unique technology, thus there is no single point of failure.
